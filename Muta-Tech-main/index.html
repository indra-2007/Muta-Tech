GenomeScan AI — Documentation & Architecture
How to Use the Website

Welcome to GenomeScan AI, a tool designed to predict the pathogenicity and disease association of genetic variants using state-of-the-art transformer models.

  • Input: Enter a genomic coordinate (e.g., Chromosome 17, Position 43094464) and the Ref/Alt alleles in the sidebar. You can also use the Quick Examples to load known variants instantly.
  • Analyze: Click "Analyze Variant". The system will fetch the surrounding DNA sequence (±64 base pairs) from the GRCh38 reference genome.
  • Results: The main dashboard will display the Pathogenicity Score (Benign vs. Pathogenic), the Top 5 Disease Associations, the Attention Heatmap on the DNA sequence, and Variant Statistics (Entropy, GC content, etc.).
Understanding the Outputs

The dashboard presents several distinct pieces of analysis:

  • Pathogenicity Score: A confidence score from 0 to 100 indicating how damaging the variant is likely to be. It is divided into bands: Benign, Likely Benign, VUS (Variant of Uncertain Significance), Likely Pathogenic, and Pathogenic.
  • Disease Prediction: The AI predicts which of the 15 supported disease categories (e.g., Cystic Fibrosis, Breast/Ovarian Cancer, Arrhythmia) the variant is most strongly associated with.
  • DNA Sequence Viewer & Attention: Shows the DNA sequence around the mutation. The heatmap background indicates the model's attention weights. Brighter red areas are where the AI is "looking" most strongly to make its decision.
  • Statistics: Includes Transition/Transversion types, Shannon Entropy (sequence complexity), GC Content, and CpG Context (methylation hotspots).
Deep Dive: Backend Model Architecture

Our backend operates on a Dual-Model Pipeline built entirely around DNABERT-2.

1. What is DNABERT-2?

Unlike standard language models that read English, DNABERT-2 is a 117-million parameter transformer pre-trained on billions of base pairs of multi-species DNA. It uses Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) to tokenize DNA into meaningful sub-sequences rather than treating it base-by-base. This allows it to learn the grammar and structural motifs of genomics.

2. The Dual-Model Approach

We fine-tuned the base DNABERT-2 model on the ClinVar dataset (a public database of human variations and their health relationships) to create two specialized heads:

  • Model 1 (Pathogenicity): A binary classifier (Benign vs. Pathogenic).
  • Model 2 (Disease Categorization): A 15-class multi-label classifier trained to predict specific phenotypic categories (e.g., Epilepsy, Cardiomyopathy, Blood Disorder).
3. Genomic Context & Biological Priors Enhancement

Because raw AI models can be noisy and undertrained on rare variants, our inference pipeline heavily blends the raw neural network output with hardcoded Biological Priors to maximize accuracy:

Score = (0.35 × AI_Raw_Output) + (0.65 × Biological_Context)

The Biological Context is calculated dynamically for every variant using:

  • Gene-Disease Mapping: We map well-studied genes directly to their diseases (e.g., CFTR → Cystic Fibrosis). The final disease prediction is a blend of 40% raw AI probability and 60% biological prior. This ensures that well-known genes surface the correct disease reliably, while unknown genes fall back completely on the AI's pure sequence analysis.
  • Gene Pathogenicity Prior: Some genes (like TP53 or BRCA1) are naturally more sensitive to mutations than others. We apply a base risk weight based on the gene.
  • CpG Boost: A CpG site (Cytosine followed by Guanine) is a known mutation hotspot due to methylation and deamination. If the model detects the mutation is at a CpG site, it slightly boosts the pathogenicity risk.
  • GC Deviation: We calculate the GC% of the 129-bp window. If it deviates heavily from the human average (~42%), it indicates a highly constrained region, modifying the risk score.
